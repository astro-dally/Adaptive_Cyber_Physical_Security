01 — The Problem

Cyber-physical systems
can't detect what they've
never seen before.

Traditional intrusion detection relies on known attack signatures. When a new, never-before-seen threat appears — a zero-day attack — these systems go silent.

Zero-Day Blindness Open-World Threat Evolving Adversaries
🔒
Signature-Based IDS
Matches known fingerprints. Fast and precise, but completely blind to novel attacks.
📊
Anomaly-Based IDS
Learns "normal" behavior. Can theoretically detect zero-days, but floods analysts with false positives.
🎯
Our Goal
Combine supervised classification with anomaly detection for adaptive, zero-day-aware security.

03 — Literature Review

What the research tells us —
and where the gap is.

IDS Evolution

From static firewalls to continuous monitoring. Early systems were built for enterprise LANs — modern CPS spans cloud, IoT, and industrial control.

ML in Cybersecurity

Supervised models like Random Forest achieve near-perfect accuracy on known attacks. But "known" is the key word — they memorize, not generalize.

Anomaly Detection

One-Class SVMs and autoencoders frame detection as boundary estimation. They don't need attack labels — just a model of what "normal" looks like.

⚠️ The Gap

Most papers evaluate on random train/test splits — leaking future attack knowledge into training. When models face true zero-day threats, recall collapses.

Our Contribution

A dual-model framework that explicitly tests for zero-day scenarios: supervised classification for known threats, anomaly detection for novel ones.

06 — Model Selection

Two models, two roles.

Cyber defense is an open-world problem. One model can't do it all. We chose two complementary approaches — one for known threats, one for the unknown.

🌲
Random Forest
Role: Supervised classification of known attack labels.
Why: Handles nonlinear boundaries in tabular data. Robust to correlated features. No scaling needed. Ensemble of 100 trees with majority voting.
Supervised 100 Trees Known Attacks
🔮
One-Class SVM
Role: Anomaly detection — trained on benign traffic only.
Why: Learns the boundary of "normal." Anything outside is flagged as anomalous. Doesn't need attack labels — ideal for zero-day detection.
Semi-Supervised RBF Kernel Zero-Day
💡 This dual-model approach is supported by recent literature — combining discriminative classifiers with anomaly detectors provides both precision on known threats and resilience to novel attacks.

08 — Results

What the models revealed.

Random Forest dominates known attacks. One-Class SVM catches what it can't. Neither alone is sufficient — together, they form an adaptive defense.

Random Forest Takeaway
Near-perfect precision and recall on seen attack types (DoS-Hulk). But conservative on zero-day attacks — tends to classify novel threats as benign because it has never learned their patterns.
One-Class SVM Takeaway
Catches anomalous behavior from novel attack families without ever seeing attack data in training. Trade-off: higher false positive rate on benign traffic, but recovers true zero-days that RF misses entirely.

🛡️ Key Insight

Known-attack recognition and zero-day detection are fundamentally different tasks. Adaptive cyber-physical defense needs both — a classifier for what you know, and an anomaly detector for what you don't.
